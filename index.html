What Does This Project Do?

In our dataset, there is information on neighborhood, neighborhood group, room type, minimum number of days, and more that correlate with each other in intriguing ways. Our goal is to predict the price of an Airbnb listing in New York City, which will be measured in US dollars.

Why This Project?

Currently, it is very popular to place a property on the Airbnb market to make some extra cash and even have a career if one owns a very nice property. Given this trend, we were inspired to create a machine learning model that can help potential hosts and tourists alike. It can help potential hosts decide whether it would be profitable to place their property on the market given its location, room type, and more. As for the tourists, they can use our model to verify that the renting price is reasonable. Another final justification was that this was a viable data set, large enough for us to ensure reasonably accurate results, but not so large that our computers could still handle it without crashing.

Visualizations


Scatterplot: Review and Price

The majority of prices and reviews is under $1000 and less than 75 reviews. The Bronx (orange) has the cheapest listings while Manhattan (red) has the most expensive listings. As for the other boroughs, Staten Island (purple), Queens (green), and Brooklyn (blue) had increasing number of reviews and prices in that order.


Scatterplot: Availability and Price

Initially, we thought that availability would have a distinct effect on the price because of seasonal demand. However, we were wrong. In this scatter plot, we can see that it does not matter how available the listing is as it does not affect the price. This graph also reaffirms that the most expensive listings are in Manhattan while the cheapest listings are in the Bronx.

Machine Learning


Linear Regression: Results and Explanation

First, we tried a baseline linear regression model. This model basically uses a line to predict the price of the Airbnb. Linear regression assumes that all features have a linear relation with price of an Airbnb in New York City, so we can fit a line of best fit to predict future values. After testing the model with our dataset, we got a root mean square error of 74 which isn't great as most of our values got to around 40 and the higher the RMSE is, the worse it is. However, it's still not terrible.

Random Forest Regression: Results and Explanation

The Random Forest Regression model uses multiple decision trees with random decisions to create outputs and trains itself on which ones work. The RMSE we got from the model was 43.8 which is pretty good but not the best we got from any of the models.

LightGBM: Results and Explanation

The Light Gradient Boosting Machine model also known as LightBGM, is very similar to the random forest regression model except it uses the outputs of its trees for inputs of other trees. Its RMSE was 44 which isn't terrible, but we had better RMSEs from other models.
